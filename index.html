Overview

From the catalog: Introduction to the study of how software controls the critical hardware components of a computer's architecture, the CPU and RAM. Topics include development of C language programs, comparison of high-order procedural languages to machine language programs, CPU organization and functionality at the register/bus level, instruction formats, and development of machine language and assembly level programs using multiple addressing modes, flow-of-control branching and subroutine calls. Prerequisites: CMS 170.

A better description: Computer science is about so much more than programming! In this class, we really get to get under the hood and see how a computer really works. There's a beauty to the hardware level and really understanding how a machine can run the code you write. This requires understanding bits and bytes, how a program can be translated from a high-level language into something the computer can understand, and how both data and instructions are stored and accessed from memory.

Honor Code

Membership in the student body of Rollins College carries with it an obligation, and requires a commitment, to act with honor in all things. The student commitment to uphold the values of honor - honesty, trust, respect, fairness, and responsibility - particularly manifests itself in two public aspects of student life. First, as part of the admission process to the College, students agree to commit themselves to the Honor Code. Then, as part of the matriculation process during Orientation, students sign a more detailed pledge to uphold the Honor Code and to conduct themselves honorably in all their activities, both academic and social, as a Rollins student. A student signature on the following pledge is a binding commitment by the student that lasts for his or her entire tenure at Rollins College:
The development of the virtues of Honor and Integrity are integral to a Rollins College education and to membership in the Rollins College community. Therefore, I, a student of Rollins College, pledge to show my commitment to these virtues by abstaining from any lying, cheating, or plagiarism in my academic endeavors and by behaving responsibly, respectfully and honorably in my social life and in my relationships with others. This pledge is reinforced every time a student submits work for academic credit as his/her own. Students shall add to the paper, quiz, test, lab report, etc., the handwritten signed statement:

"On my honor, I have not given, nor received, nor witnessed any unauthorized assistance on this work."
Material submitted electronically should contain the pledge; submission implies signing the pledge.

How do I interpret the Honor Code?
In the context of this class, my interpretation of the Honor Code pledge is that the work you submit for every assignment shall be your own original creation. This means that any item you submit must be written by you and you alone, or in an assigned group project by your group alone. Copying a solution from fellow class members, previous class members, books, or the Internet is a violation of this policy and will constitute academic misconduct.

What is acceptable collaboration?
It is acceptable to talk about problems and solutions with other members of the class in general terms. If you're talking about how you might solve a problem, or discussing how to use features of C, that is generally allowed. Copying or sharing code, even if you change the code in small ways, is definitely not permitted.

It is acceptable to consult books and Internet resources like Stack Overflow for general programming questions---I consider this to be documentation---but it is not acceptable to copy entire solutions from other sources.

How do I document my collaboration?
You must thoroughly document any other students or outside materials you consult in the course of completing an assignment. For example, if you consult Stack Overflow, you should copy and paste that URL into the README.md file in your GitHub repo for that assignment. If you consult with another student, you should include their name as well. There is no penalty for appropriate collaboration, and documenting other students and/or sources consulted will not impact your grade on the assignment.

Please ask if you have questions about what is permissible!

Credit Hour Statement

Rollins College offers four-credit-hour courses that provide three (50-minute) hours of direct or indirect instructional contact. The value of four credit hours reflects the substantial individual attention each student receives from instructors as well as additional out-of-class activities. Faculty require that students undertake at least 7.5 (60-minute) hours of outside work per week, averaged over the course's duration and equaling two and one-half (2.5) 60-minute hours of outside work for every one (50 minute) hour of scheduled class time. In this course, the additional outside-of-class expectations are substantial programming projects, technical reading, and written assignments utilizing the computer systems concepts presented in class.
